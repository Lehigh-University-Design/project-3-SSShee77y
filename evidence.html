A Challenging Search

After the hijacking of Northwest Orient Airlines Flight 305, the FBI recovered various pieces of evidence, including 66 latent fingerprints, Cooper's black clip-on tie, tie clip, and two of the four parachutes. One of the parachutes had been opened, with three shroud lines cut from the canopy. FBI agents conducted interviews with eyewitnesses in Portland, Seattle, and Reno, and developed composite sketches based on their accounts.

Local police and FBI agents began questioning possible suspects immediately after the hijacking. A citizen named D. B. Cooper in Portland was interviewed but quickly eliminated as a suspect. A reporter's error led to the hijacker being mistakenly referred to as "D. B. Cooper," a pseudonym that stuck.

The search for Cooper was challenging due to various factors, including uncertainties about the aircraft's airspeed, environmental conditions along the flight path, and Cooper's actions during the jump. Despite efforts by Air Force F-106 pilots and others, no one witnessed Cooper jumping from the aircraft, and no deployed parachute was detected. The darkness, limited visibility, cloud cover, and lack of ground lighting further complicated search efforts.

The End?

On July 8, 2016, the FBI redirected resources allocated to the D.B. Cooper case in order to focus on other investigative priorities.

During the course of the 45-year NORJAK investigation, the FBI exhaustively reviewed all credible leads, coordinated between multiple field offices to conduct searches, collected all available evidence, and interviewed all identified witnesses. All physical evidence obtained during the course of the investigation is held at the FBI Headquarters in Washington, D.C for historical purposes.
